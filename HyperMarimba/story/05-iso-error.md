In the previous comparison we only run the geodesics for length $25$. The reason why we stoped there is that, after that point, the scores start to diverge. This is what one would expect. Numerically, two numbers which differ by less than $10^{-16}$ are declared to be the same. On the other hand, in hyperbolic geometry, geodesics corresponding to two vectors which are at that distance might reach reach distance $1$ in less than length $37$. In some sense, getting almost identical results up to length $25$ should be taken as proof that the calculations are extremely precise. Seeing them diverge afterwards is proof of honesty! 😉

When you press _Run Both_, you see in the first pannel the scores, up to length $50$, of two geodesics that in the absence of computational errors would give you identical melodies. In the next pannel you see how the error evolves (measured in a way that made calculations easy). This second panel might look at first pretty confussing because the error seems to go from $0$ to behaving like a random walk in no time. The reason for this becomes however clear when we display it in $\log$-scale, where is just grows steadily and linearly until it reaches some point when it becomes random. This is hyperbolic dynamics at its best!
