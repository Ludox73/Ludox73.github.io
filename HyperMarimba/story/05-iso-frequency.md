Given that the errors compound so fast, you might be wondering what is the point of working with longer geodesics, and what is the point of the numerical estimations for frequencies. 

Well, given that up to relatively large length (length $25$) the calculations are very precise, it follows that the path we are following is a _quasi-geodesic_ with very good constants. Stability of quasi-geodesics in hyperbolic geometry implies that such _quasi-geodesics fellow-travel a unique geodesic_. The melody we are recording is, with very small errors, the melody of that geodesic. Moreover, if the errors are random, as one might as well suppose, the obtained geodesic is as random as the initial one. You can think of this as follows: _when we select by random a vector, the vector in question feels too lazy to run himself playing music everytime he encounters $\Gamma$ and instead passes the chore of doing so to another randomly chosen vector in his immediate surrounding_. 

Since the frequencies of motifs are independent of the chosen random vector, involving this lazy middleman in the process has no effect for the frequency estimation. We exhibit this phenomenon by comparing the frequencies of the same motives in our two isomelodic marimbas.
